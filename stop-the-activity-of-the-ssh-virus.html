Stop the activity of the SSH virus

  • Apr 17, 2025
  • Categories: Linux
  • Tags: #ssh

A Hacker’s Behavior Often Imitates a Connection Issue

Sometimes, a hacker’s behavior can mimic problems with your internet connection. For example, your router might be working, all the indicator lights show that the internet is connected, but the speed is extremely slow and you can’t load any pages. Maybe your device loses connection several times a day. Occasionally, you might even notice the modem shutting down without any action on your part.

Of course, the first thing most people do is call their internet provider. But in these cases, the response is usually the same: "We're providing full internet service on our end."

From that point on, you may find yourself dealing with a highly skilled hacker. And they—he or she—can toy with you however they like.

Your first step should be to check the active processes on your device. In many cases, running processes can reveal valuable details about remote hacker activity. Once you understand what's happening, you can either resolve the issue or at least minimize the impact.

In most cases, remote access is established through SSH. Here’s what you can do:

Open a terminal as an administrator

su -

Or

sudo -i   # if you're using Kali Linux

Terminate all SSH agent processes

killall ssh-agent

Check for active SSH connections

ps -U root | grep ssh

Or alternatively

ps -ef | grep ssh

Once you identify the hacker’s SSH process by its PID (Process ID), you can try to terminate it:

kill -INT [PID]

You can also use (root needed):

lsof /path/to/target
kill -9 [PID]
netstat -tnpa | grep 'ESTABLISHED.*sshd'

Note: This method didn’t work for me, but it might work for you.

Final Tip:

Keep your processes clean! Regularly monitoring what's running on your system is a good habit, especially if you suspect unusual activity.
